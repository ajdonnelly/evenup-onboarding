Customer onboarding & change management for legal AI

I help law firms move from legacy ways of working toward practical, AI-enabled operations.

For more than a decade, I have worked inside plaintiff-side and complex litigation practices, helping manage the systems and workflows that keep a firm moving: case management, billing, docketing, discovery, document organization, and the day-to-day coordination behind active matters. Much of that work has involved the less visible but essential challenge of helping busy legal teams adopt new tools and turn them into part of their actual practice.

I have learned that software does not create value on its own. Adoption is won or lost in the everyday work of the people closest to the file: case managers, paralegals, intake staff, and attorneys who need to trust that a new process will make their work better rather than simply add another step.

I now focus that experience on AI adoption for personal-injury firms. My approach is grounded in a recent legal-AI pilot within a medical-malpractice practice and starts with the firm’s real workflows: identify one high-friction task, test an AI-assisted process with the right guardrails, measure what changes, and build from there.

About

I understand law-firm adoption from the inside

For more than a decade I ran the daily operations of a boutique plaintiff-side employment and corporate-law firm. I handled many elements of the practice end to end: intake, client communication, case and matter management, discovery, document production, vendor and software coordination, and billing. I know how a firm actually runs, where the most significant pain points are, and what it takes to get professionals to trust and adopt a new way of working.

Across my career, I have also been part of repeated waves of technology adoption and system-of-record change. Early on, that meant helping digitize hard-copy files and streamline billing workflows. At a larger firm, it included supporting the implementation of automated billing processes across a roughly 50-attorney practice. In plaintiff-side litigation, it meant working alongside firm leadership and colleagues to evaluate, pilot, and adopt new systems as the practice evolved — from billing software transitions such as WinVantage to Clio, to multiple litigation-platform changes, including Clio to CaseFleet, and the move from PACER-based tracking toward a more automated digital docketing workflow through DocketBird.

More recently I have done that adoption work for AI. As a senior litigation paralegal I joined my firm’s AI committee and an enterprise pilot integrating tools like Microsoft Copilot and Filevine’s Lois into real legal workflows — testing them against live tasks (including close QC of AI-generated medical chronologies), evaluating output, documenting workflows, and co-leading training for colleagues from staff to partners. My graduate training in biomedical visualization is, at its core, about making complex things understandable. This fall I begin the University of Hawaiʻi Executive J.D. program focused on AI law and governance.

Change management

Adoption is change management, not a software install

Getting a firm into proactive AI is not a configuration task — it is behavior change, role by role, earned through deep engagement with the people doing the work. EvenUp already frames this well: set the vision, empower the firm, make the change stick. That is exactly the work I do, plus the one thing that decides everything in legal AI — earning trust in the output.

01

Set the vision

Align leadership before rollout on what success looks like — the outcomes the firm actually cares about, from time on desk to policy-limit recovery.

02

Empower the firm

Identify champions, redefine roles, and show each person — managing partner to case manager — how AI makes their work more fulfilling, not threatened.

03

Make the change stick

Repeatable training and programs that turn first use into durable habit across the whole firm — not a one-time demo that fades by Friday.

04

Earn trust in the output

Coach the review-against-source habit on AI demands and chronologies, so confidence is earned — the difference between a firm that logs in and one that relies on it.

Why this work

Why legal AI, and why EvenUp

A point of view: in PI, adoption lives with the support staff.

So much of a personal-injury practice runs on people who aren’t attorneys — case managers, paralegals, and intake. Adoption is won or lost with them. Train the partners and forget the staff who do the volume work, and the rollout stalls by Friday. I build for the whole firm, and I start with the roles most onboarding plans overlook — because they’re the ones who turn a new tool into daily practice.

I am drawn to legal AI because I believe it is one of the most important ways to serve innovation in law firms now.

For all of the discussion about disruption, the real opportunity is not simply to introduce new technology into legal practice. It is to help firms use it in ways that reduce friction, expand capacity, improve the quality and consistency of their work, and create more room for the human parts of practice that clients actually need: judgment, communication, strategy, advocacy, and care. Having worked inside firms through multiple waves of technology adoption, I have seen how much potential is lost when a tool is treated as the solution rather than as part of a larger change process.

Why EvenUp

EvenUp is compelling to me because it is building for a part of legal practice I know intimately: personal-injury firms trying to turn overwhelming volumes of medical records, case documents, and deadlines into stronger cases, faster settlements, and better client service. Medical chronologies are not an abstract use case — they shape early case evaluation, damages analysis, negotiation, and ultimately the story a firm can tell about a client’s injury. That is a problem I have worked to solve from inside firms, and one EvenUp is solving at scale.

I am also drawn to EvenUp’s multimodal potential. My background in biomedical visualization has made me deeply interested in how people learn from and make sense of complex information — across records, images, timelines, and audio — and that is exactly what plaintiff teams and their clients need to build a shared understanding of a difficult case. I would be glad to bring my experience in legal operations, medical-legal communication, system-of-record change, and AI enablement to a team helping firms make technology genuinely useful for the people who depend on it.
