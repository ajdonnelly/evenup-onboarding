## CHAPTER 1 — When a case is ready for a MedChron

**[Statement block]** *Learning objective:* Identify when a matter is ready for a MedChron and prepare a complete, usable source packet.

**[Text block]**
A MedChron is only as good as the packet it's built from. The question isn't "who should use MedChrons?" — it's *"at what moment in this case would a verified, searchable chronology change what someone does next?"* For most matters that's shortly after records are collected and before demand strategy, treatment-gap follow-up, or attorney case review.

**[Process block]** — Readiness checklist
1. Medical records collected for the treatment window
2. Bills present and matched to providers
3. Provider list verified (names, facilities, dates)
4. Treatment date range confirmed; obvious gaps noted
5. Correct matter selected; files named to the firm's convention

**[Text block]**
At Lakefront Injury Group, the pilot starts with standard motor-vehicle and premises matters that meet these criteria — and holds back catastrophic or heavily fragmented files until the workflow is trusted.

**[Knowledge check — scenario]**
Q: Records are in, but two providers from the police report and intake aren't in the file yet. Is the matter ready to submit?
- A) Yes — submit and add the rest later
- B) No — complete the provider set first, or flag the known gap before submitting ✅
- C) Yes — the AI will find them
Feedback (correct): Submitting an incomplete packet produces an incomplete chronology. Complete the set, or flag the gap so it's tracked, not missed.

## CHAPTER 2 — How to review a MedChron

**[Statement block]** *Learning objective:* Use the chronology to validate key medical events, identify missing information, and verify the story of treatment against the source.

**[Text block]**
Reviewing isn't re-reading every page — it's spot-checking the chain at the points that matter most, using the timeline, filters, and exhibit links.

**[Accordion or numbered list block]** — the review pass
1. **Orient on the timeline.** Scan the arc of treatment — onset, key diagnoses, procedures, provider sequence, and the end of care.
2. **Verify the high-stakes facts.** Click the citations on the injury, the major diagnoses, and the largest bills. Each should land on the record that supports it.
3. **Filter and cross-check.** Filter by date or provider; compare the chronology's visit count and providers against the records you have.
4. **Hunt for gaps.** Treatment gaps, missing records, and pre-existing conditions an adjuster or defense will attack — surface them, don't bury them.
5. **Spot discrepancies.** Transposed dates, merged encounters, "resolved" vs. "ongoing," a minor note read as a major finding. Verify severity language against the physician's own words.

**[Flashcard grid block]** — catch these
- **Transposed/wrong dates** → spot-check dates on key events; watch month/day flips.
- **Merged encounters** → compare visit count to the records for that provider.
- **Missing records** → check the provider list against intake and the bills.
- **Mischaracterized severity** → verify against the physician's wording.
- **Broken citation** → if the linked page doesn't say it, fix it.

**[Knowledge check — multiple response]**
Q: Which belong in the review pass? (Select all)
- Verify high-stakes facts against citations ✅
- Filter and cross-check visit counts ✅
- Re-type the records by hand ❌
- Surface treatment gaps and pre-existing conditions ✅
- Verify severity language against the source ✅
Feedback: Verify smartly at the highest-stakes points — don't redo the work by hand.

## CHAPTER 3 — Turning chronology findings into action

**[Statement block]** *Learning objective:* Translate chronology findings into concrete case-management and attorney-review actions.

**[Text block]**
A chronology isn't a static deliverable — it's a to-do list for the case. Every gap and discrepancy you found becomes an action with an owner.

**[Numbered list block]** — from finding to action
1. **Missing records** → open a records request; log it for the case manager to track.
2. **Treatment gaps** → flag for client/provider follow-up before they cost case value.
3. **Pre-existing conditions or causation questions** → escalate to the attorney; these can affect case theory and damages.
4. **Attorney case-review prep** → note the questions the chronology helps answer (severity, timeline, valuation).
5. **Document it** → record findings in the firm's existing system so nothing lives only in someone's head.

**[Statement block]**
> Escalate anything that could affect causation, damages, or case theory. That judgment is the human work the tool exists to support — not replace.

**[Knowledge check — scenario]**
Q: The chronology shows a six-week gap between the ER visit and the first follow-up, and a prior back injury noted once. What do you do?
- A) Nothing — it's in the chronology
- B) Flag the treatment gap for follow-up and escalate the pre-existing condition to the attorney ✅
- C) Delete the prior-injury note so it doesn't hurt the case
- D) Wait for the attorney to notice
Feedback (correct): Surface and route both. Gaps and pre-existing conditions are exactly what the defense will probe — better caught now, by you.
