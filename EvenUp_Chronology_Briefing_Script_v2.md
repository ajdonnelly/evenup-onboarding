## SCRIPT

**HOST A:** Let me start with the uncomfortable one. A personal-injury firm exists to hold professionals accountable when their work falls below the standard of care. So what's the nightmare when *that* firm starts using AI?

**HOST B:** That they commit their own version of it. And it's not hypothetical — there are already lawyers who've been sanctioned for filing briefs with citations an AI just invented. For a med-mal firm, the irony writes itself. So the fear underneath all of this is real: if I lean on this tool and it's wrong, and I don't catch it, that's on me.

**HOST A:** Right. And in this work, "wrong" isn't a typo. Walk me through what's actually at stake in a medical chronology.

**HOST B:** The chronology is the backbone of the case. It's the timeline of every diagnosis, every visit, every procedure, pulled from sometimes thousands of pages of records. The whole theory — causation, damages, what the treating physician should have known and when — hangs off that timeline. So if the AI transposes a date, or merges two encounters into one, or skips the one nursing note where the patient's vitals first went sideways…

**HOST A:** …you've potentially lost the case before you've even drafted the demand.

**HOST B:** Exactly. And the error doesn't announce itself. The chronology looks clean and confident. That's what makes it dangerous — you'd catch a sloppy output. It's the polished, plausible, slightly-wrong one that's the real risk.

**HOST A:** So this is where you tell me the technology solves that.

**HOST B:** No — and that's the honest answer the good firms actually want. The technology doesn't remove the risk. A discipline does. The one habit that makes any of this safe is: **review against the source.** You don't re-read all thousand pages — that defeats the purpose. You spot-check the chain where it matters most. The injury, the major diagnoses, the biggest bills. Click the citation. If the linked page doesn't say what the timeline claims, you fix it before it ships.

**HOST A:** So the AI does the extraction, and the human does the judgment.

**HOST B:** The tool drafts; the human decides. That's the whole philosophy in six words. And here's the part people miss — that's not a demotion for the paralegal. It moves them off the soul-crushing manual retyping and onto the work that wins cases: gap-spotting, verifying severity against the doctor's own words, catching the pre-existing condition the defense is going to hammer. The expertise matters *more*, not less.

**HOST A:** Okay — that's accuracy. But before a firm gets there, the risk people in the room have a different first question. Let's say it out loud.

**HOST B:** Security. And rightly so. This is some of the most sensitive data that exists — protected health information moving between health systems, insurers, and counsel, wrapped in privilege. So the questions are non-negotiable: Where does the data live? Is it encrypted? Who can see it? And the big one — does the vendor train its models on our clients' records?

**HOST A:** And that's where consumer AI tools and purpose-built legal platforms part ways.

**HOST B:** Hard line between them. Pasting a client's records into a free public chatbot to "just get a quick summary" — that's the move that should make a firm's stomach drop. A purpose-built platform is a different animal: data isolation, encryption, access controls, audit logs, and a commitment not to train on your data. Strong prompting and strong governance travel together. Neither substitutes for the other.

**HOST A:** So: trust the output, and protect the data. Two gates. But let's be real about why a firm actually signs the contract. They're a business.

**HOST B:** And a particular kind — contingency. They front the costs, do years of work, and don't get paid until the case resolves. So pitch them on "efficiency" and it lands flat. What they hear is cash flow.

**HOST A:** So translate it into their P&L.

**HOST B:** The real story is speed and capacity. A chronology that took days by hand and now takes hours means the demand goes out sooner. The demand out sooner means the settlement comes sooner. And the settlement sooner means the fee — the thing that funds the entire firm — lands sooner. Same headcount, more cases moving through the pipe. That's not a productivity story. It's a growth story.

**HOST A:** And a quality story too, presumably — a better-supported demand.

**HOST B:** That's the underrated part. When the chronology is thorough and every fact is tied to a record, the demand is harder to brush off. The adjuster can't wave it away. You're not just faster — you're more persuasive. And in this work, that shows up directly in settlement value.

**HOST A:** So if you're the firm weighing this, the arc is: can I trust it, is it safe, is it worth it.

**HOST B:** And the answer to all three depends less on the software than on how you roll it out. The firms that win don't treat it as a login. They build the review habit, they put the guardrails in first, and they let the people doing the work see that it makes their jobs better. Adoption is behavior change. The tool is the easy part.

**HOST A:** The tool drafts.

**HOST B:** The human decides. That's the whole thing.
