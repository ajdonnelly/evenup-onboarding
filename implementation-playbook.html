From pilot to practice: a 90-day MedChrons adoption

A role-based adoption operating model for a personal-injury firm — shown not as a generic firmwide rollout, but as one bounded, measurable pilot of a single high-value workflow: building and reviewing medical chronologies in EvenUp. Adoption starts with the work already happening inside the firm, not with the technology.

The setting & the bet

One workflow, one cohort, real evidence

The firm (fictional)

A realistic but invented setting, so every artifact in this playbook shares one concrete story.

Lakefront Injury Group — a 45-person plaintiff-side PI firm: three offices, 12 attorneys, 16 paralegals and case managers, and a growing pre-litigation practice handling motor-vehicle, premises, and moderate-to-complex injury matters. Chronology creation is inconsistent across teams, attorney review varies, and records gaps surface late in demand prep.

The bet

Why a bounded pilot beats a firmwide plan.

Take one high-friction, high-frequency workflow — medical chronologies — redesign it around Lakefront’s real case operations, test it with a small cohort, measure the result, and expand only after the team has evidence the new process improves their work. The MedChron is the first repeatable adoption playbook, not the last product rollout.

The 90-day arc

Discover → Configure → Pilot → Measure → Expand

Five phases, mapped onto EvenUp’s own change-management model — set the vision, empower the firm, make the change stick. Each phase has an exit condition; you don’t move on until it’s met.

1 Discover & baseline · Days 1–15 · Set the vision

Start by learning how the firm actually works, not by training. Leadership kickoff; 30-minute role interviews (attorneys, paralegals, case managers, records staff); walk through 3–5 recent matters; map the current chronology process and handoffs; choose the cohort; capture baseline cycle-time and effort; surface adoption risks before they become resistance.

Exit: current-state map, stakeholder map, pilot charter, baseline scorecard, risk register, case-eligibility criteria, and a short communications plan.

2 Design & configure · Days 16–30 · Set the vision → Empower

Turn product knowledge into Lakefront’s own process: case eligibility, submission readiness, paralegal review, attorney review, case-management follow-up, and quality/escalation. Configure EvenUp to the firm’s CMS and SOPs; build role-specific quick-reference guides.

Exit: future-state workflow map, RACI, review checklist, escalation matrix, pilot case roster, and launch communications.

3 Run the controlled pilot · Days 31–60 · Empower

The heart of the engagement, deliberately designed — not “turn it on and wait.” On-site training week (champions first), go live on the eligible matters, daily huddles the first two weeks then weekly, a shared issue log, and a closed-loop review on every completed matter. Pair every user with the habit that protects trust: review AI output against the source.

Exit: every role trained; first AI chronologies produced, QC’d, and used by attorneys; champions running point inside the firm.

4 Measure & refine · Days 61–75 · Make the change stick

Move from anecdotes to evidence. Build the pilot scorecard (operational and human measures), tune the workflow rules against what the data shows, and prepare an evidence-based expansion decision.

Exit: pilot scorecard vs. baseline, a refined SOP, and a drafted recommendation grounded in evidence, not enthusiasm.

5 Operationalize & expand · Days 76–90 · Make the change stick

A disciplined expansion decision — continue, refine, expand, or pause. Ship v2 training, stand up a firm champion network, set a 30/60/90 reinforcement plan, and open a standing feedback loop to EvenUp’s product team.

Exit: outcomes report, v2 SOP & training, champion guide, expansion recommendation, reinforcement plan, and a product feedback loop.

Pilot scope

A small, deliberately chosen cohort

Bounded on purpose — wins are visible, issues are contained, and the test is fair.

01

The cohort

One office / one practice group: 2 attorneys, 4 paralegals, 1 case-manager & records liaison, and a named firm champion — across roughly 15–25 matters.

02

Include

Standard motor-vehicle and premises matters with records collected and a defined treatment window.

03

Exclude at first

Catastrophic injuries, highly fragmented records, and matters in heavy active treatment — unless complexity is intentionally part of the test.

04

Cadence

A weekly 30-minute pilot huddle, one shared issue log, and a structured review of completed matters each week.

The discipline that makes it real

The closed-loop review

For every completed MedChron in the pilot, the cohort answers seven questions. That is what turns training into operational learning.

  1. Was it submitted at the right stage of the case?
  2. Was the source packet ready — records, bills, providers, dates?
  3. Did the chronology surface something genuinely useful?
  4. Did the paralegal need to correct, supplement, or clarify anything?
  5. Did the attorney use it in evaluation, demand prep, negotiation, or strategy?
  6. Were missing records or treatment gaps identified?
  7. Would the team use it again on the next eligible matter?

Measurement discipline

In 90 days, success is not proving every downstream business outcome — settlement lift, for instance, is real at scale but hard to isolate quickly. Success is establishing a trusted, repeatable behavior with measurable operational value and a clear path to scale.

Honest about friction

Where adoption stalls — and the expansion call

What actually derails a rollout

Legal teams won’t trust a process they can’t verify. People fear losing role status — or being blamed for errors in an output they didn’t draft. A polished chronology doesn’t automatically fit the firm’s handoffs. A pilot cohort that’s too large or too complex fails for the wrong reasons. Training attendance is not behavior change. And adoption slips once the initial support fades without clear ownership. I can name these honestly because I’ve lived them — medical information isn’t just data; it becomes case theory, damages, and advocacy.
